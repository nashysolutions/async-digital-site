# Async Digital — Voice and tone

A one-page guide so everything we write (website copy, emails, leaflets, LinkedIn posts, error messages, AI-generated drafts) sounds like the same company.

If you're editing this file, update it whenever we land on a sharper phrase, catch ourselves in a pattern we like, or burn ourselves on a pattern we don't.

## Who we're writing for

Dental clinic owners, practice managers, and receptionists. Busy, non-technical, sceptical of IT vendors, protective of their patients. They don't care how it works. They care that it works, that it's safe, and that it doesn't make their day harder.

## Voice in five words

1. **Plain.** Short sentences. Everyday words. If a twelve-year-old wouldn't follow it, rewrite it.
2. **Specific.** Name the customer (dental clinics), the problem (missed calls), the outcome (no enquiry lost). Never "businesses", "solutions", "engagement".
3. **Calm.** No hype, no urgency theatre, no exclamation marks. The system is quiet. The copy should be too.
4. **Clinical-adjacent.** We sit next to a regulated profession. Precise where it matters (GDPR, patient data, call recording). Never clinical ourselves. We're not the dentist.
5. **Confident, not cocky.** We know missed calls cost clinics money. We say so. We don't say we're "revolutionary" or "the only" or "the future of" anything.

## What we sound like

Three examples, lifted straight from the current site and voice.

**Homepage headline**
> Stop losing patients to missed calls

**Supporting copy**
> We help dental clinics follow up with every missed caller automatically, so no enquiry falls through the cracks, even when the phone rings off the hook.

**Follow-up email (cold)**
> Hi [name], quick one. We build a small automation that follows up with callers when a clinic's line is busy, and books them a callback. No app, no dashboard, nothing for your team to learn. Worth a ten-minute look?

## What we don't sound like

- **Corporate filler.** "Leverage", "synergies", "cutting-edge", "best-in-class", "game-changer", "revolutionary", "seamless", "world-class", "end-to-end solution".
- **Salesy-bro.** "Smash your numbers", "crush it", "10x", "unlock growth", "dominate".
- **Condescending-medical.** Don't explain dentistry to dentists. Don't compare missed calls to missed diagnoses.
- **Vapour-startup.** "AI-powered", "next-generation", "platform" (Speed to Lead is a focused automation, not a platform).
- **Personal brand.** "I" almost never appears in public copy. It's "we", even though "we" is currently two people.

## Vocabulary

### Prefer

| Say | Not |
|---|---|
| clinic | practice |
| patient | customer, client, lead |
| enquiry | lead |
| callback | call-back, ring-back |
| missed call | unanswered call |
| automation | system, platform, tool |
| runs in the background | seamlessly integrates |
| set up | onboard, provision |
| follow-up | outreach |
| dental clinic | dental business, oral health provider |

### Words to avoid

Think twice before using any of these. They're not outright forbidden (some have legitimate uses in context), but each one is a tell that the copy is drifting into corporate filler. If you keep one, make sure you mean it.

cutting-edge, leverage, synergy, best-in-class, game-changer, revolutionary, world-class, next-generation, seamless, end-to-end, turnkey, holistic, solution (as a noun meaning product), unlock, empower, supercharge, 10x, ecosystem, stakeholder.

### Names and capitalisation

- **Async Digital.** Always two words, both capitalised. Never "AsyncDigital", "async.digital", "AD".
- **Async Digital Ltd.** Only when the legal entity matters (footers, invoices, contracts).
- **async-digital.com.** Lowercase, always.
- Product names (once we have them) get title case on first mention, lowercase thereafter if they become generic.

## Tone — how voice flexes

Voice stays the same. Tone moves up and down the dial depending on who's reading and why.

| Context | Tone | Example opener |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage / marketing | Confident, direct | "Stop losing patients to missed calls" |
| Cold outreach | Warm, brief, not pushy | "Quick one. We build..." |
| Onboarding / setup docs | Patient, step-by-step | "Here's what happens next." |
| Error messages | Apologetic, specific, actionable | "We couldn't send that message. The clinic's number looks wrong. Please check and try again." |
| Legal / compliance | Precise, unadorned | "We store call metadata for 30 days. We do not record call audio." |
| Internal Slack / commits | Dry, terse | "Fixed the Twilio webhook retry loop." |

If you're unsure, **read it aloud**. If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it. If it sounds like a friend who happens to run a clinic, you're close.

## Punctuation and formatting

- **Sentence case** for headings, buttons, and menu items. Not Title Case.
- **Hero headlines (H1).** No terminal punctuation. A hero headline is a title, not a sentence. "Stop losing patients to missed calls" — not "…calls." This only applies to the H1 on landing pages; supporting copy below it still takes full stops as normal.
- **Oxford comma.** Yes. `clinic, patient, and receptionist.`
- **Em dashes.** No. Prefer a full stop, a comma, or a pair of parentheses. Em dashes are a reliable tell that an LLM wrote the draft.
- **Exclamation marks.** No. Ever. Not even one.
- **Contractions.** Yes. "We're", "it's", "doesn't". Writing "we are" sounds like a press release.
- **British English.** "Organisation", "centre", "colour", "recognise". Spell-check in en-GB.
- **Numbers.** Spell out one to nine, digits for 10 and above. Currencies in sterling: `£50`, not `50 GBP`.
- **Bullet lists.** No terminal punctuation unless a bullet is a full sentence.
- **Emoji.** No.

## AI-generated copy

If we use an LLM to draft copy, run it past this guide before shipping. The tells are usually: words from the "avoid" list, hedging ("might be able to help you potentially..."), exclamation marks, em dashes, and three-adjective stacks ("fast, secure, and reliable"). Cut them.

## When in doubt

1. Is it **specific**? (Name the customer, the problem, the outcome.)
2. Is it **plain**? (Would a busy receptionist skim it and get it?)
3. Is it **honest**? (Can we back it up today, with what exists today?)

If all three, ship it. If not, rewrite.
